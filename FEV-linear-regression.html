In the model we see Age, Height, Male, & Nitrate all have positive significant association. Age and height both are positive predictors, for every unit increase in height FEV increases by about 46.8 units; this is expected since older and larger people have larger lung capacity. Nitrate shows a significant positive association (~261.3), sometimes in environmental studies this is due to col-linearity (pollutants being highly correlated with each other) which can flip the sign of a coefficient. Male is binary and is associated with an average increase of 295.4 units in FEV compared to females, this holds all other variables constant.

Sulfate, PM2.5, & Elemental Carbon has a different story. Sulfate has a significant negative impact, for every unit increased in sulfate levels decreases FEV by about 679.0 units (KEY FINDING). EC and PM2.5: EC in this model doesn’t really have a statistically significant effect on FEV in this model. PM2.5 has a p-value of 0.0505 which is right on the line of significance, it does have a positive trend but isn’t strictly significant here

So from my understanding Sulfate may be the strongest predictor here, with male (sex) in close following
